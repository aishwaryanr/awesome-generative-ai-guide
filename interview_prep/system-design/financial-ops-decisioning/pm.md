# Financial-Operations Decisioning Agent: the PM interview

## The interview question

> "Our finance ops team drowns in invoice and expense review. You are the PM. Should we automate the decision, what does success look like, and how do you keep auditors and the CFO comfortable?"

The same scenario as the [engineering version](README.md), worked for an **AI Product Manager** loop. The interviewer here is testing product judgment: whether an agent should decide invoices and expenses at all, what good looks like when money is on the line, and how you keep the finance team, the auditors, and the CFO comfortable while a probabilistic model sits in the workflow. The technical design (extraction, the deterministic policy rulebook, evals, the audit hash chain) lives in the [engineering writeup](README.md). This page is the reasoning of the PM who owns the outcome.

> **Read this with your coding agent.** This case is public. Point Claude Code, Codex, or any agent at `https://github.com/aishwaryanr/awesome-generative-ai-guide/tree/main/interview_prep/system-design/financial-ops-decisioning` and have it quiz you on the product decisions, pressure-test your metrics and rollout plan, or role-play the CFO who wants to know why a model is allowed to approve a payment.

**AI product management is product management with a probabilistic core.** You still start from the user and the business problem, you still pick the smallest thing that moves a real metric, and you still design for failure. What changes is that the core component is non-deterministic, so evaluation, trust, and the human fallback move from edge cases to the center of the product. In a workflow that moves money, that shift is the whole game: a wrong decision is a real loss or a compliance finding, so the machinery around the model (policy, evidence, sign-off, and the audit trail) is the product.

## The PM spine: 6 questions for any AI product

This is the product companion to the [5-layer engineering spine](../README.md#the-spine-how-we-think-about-ai-system-design). Where the engineer reasons about the model, the wrapping layer, evals, production, and optimization, the PM reasons about whether the thing should exist and how to ship it without breaking trust. Bring these 6 questions to any AI product.

- **1 Problem and user.** Whose pain, what outcome, and whether it is worth doing at all.
- **2 Fit.** Whether AI is the right tool, whether it is good enough yet, and whether you build or buy.
- **3 Success metrics.** What you will move, the number that must not break, and the anti-metric you refuse to game.
- **4 Experience.** The probabilistic UX: trust, evidence, the review queue, and the human handoff.
- **5 Evaluation and risk.** How you know it is ready, the guardrails, and the one failure that ends the project.
- **6 Rollout.** Shadow, pilot, staged gates, and when to pull back.

Product management is composing these into a decision you can defend to a finance analyst, an engineer, and a CFO in the same meeting.

## The answer

### 1 Problem and user

Start from the problem, and reach for the technology second. This is [Problem-First](https://maven.com/aishwarya-kiriti/genai-system-design), and it is the part a weak PM skips. It is also plain product discipline: classic product discovery de-risks value and viability before a team commits to building, which is the [four big product risks](https://www.svpg.com/four-big-risks/) frame applied to an AI feature.

> **What is Problem-First?** A framework we developed and teach at [LevelUp Labs](https://levelup-labs.ai): work backwards from the business problem and the user's pain, scope hard, and only then reach for AI, choosing the smallest intervention that moves a measurable outcome. It is the spine of our [AI System Design course](https://maven.com/aishwarya-kiriti/genai-system-design).

Frame it in numbers a business owns. Accounts-payable and finance-operations analysts key invoices and expenses into an ERP, match each one against a purchase order and the policy, and approve or reject it one at a time. Volume is high and most items are routine and low-value, so the work is slow and repetitive, and it spikes hard at month-end close. Industry estimates put a manually processed invoice on the order of 10 to 20 dollars, and automated processing closer to a couple of dollars. Three users carry the pain: the analyst buried in data entry, the vendor or employee waiting to be paid, and the finance leader who owns both the spend and the controls.

Write the outcome before the system: auto-decide the clean, in-policy majority and route only genuine exceptions to a person, so analysts spend their time on judgment calls and the close lands faster. Narrow the intervention until it hurts: pick one workflow (invoice approval or expense audit), extract the facts, apply the written policy, decide approve, deny, or route-to-human, and record every decision. The agent proposes and a person disposes on anything high-impact or uncertain. An agent that tries to cover invoices, expenses, vendor onboarding, and fraud in version 1 is the over-scoping trap.

The clarifying questions you need to ask a stakeholder set every later tradeoff: what is the dollar ceiling above which a human must sign off; what is the cost of a wrong approval against the cost of a wrong denial; how much of the policy is written as hard rules against analyst judgment; how clean is the reference data (vendor master, purchase orders, prior invoices); what are the retention and segregation-of-duties requirements; and what is the promise to the finance and audit teams about oversight.

> **Real arc: MIT Sloan and Stanford GSB, 2025.** Across hundreds of thousands of transactions at 79 companies, AI cut the average monthly financial close by roughly 7.5 days and raised general-ledger granularity by about 12%, with accountants using AI as a tool alongside their judgment. The PM lesson is that the win is real and it concentrates in compressing a slow, repetitive cycle, and it holds because a human stays on the judgment calls rather than being removed from them. [[Stanford GSB](https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/insights/ai-reshaping-accounting-jobs-doing-boring-stuff)]

### 2 Fit: is AI the right tool, and do you build or buy

Capability judgment is the AI PM's signature skill: deciding whether a model belongs anywhere near the problem, and where it earns autonomy. The important call here is to split the work. Extraction (reading a messy PDF invoice or a freeform expense note and returning typed fields) is exactly the fuzzy, messy-input-to-clean-output task a model handles well. Applying the policy (is the amount over the limit, is the category reimbursable, does this invoice id already exist) is a knowable rule, so it lives in deterministic code that an auditor can read, and the model never judges it. Handing the model a decision the policy already answers exactly would turn a provable outcome into a probabilistic one and make it hard to defend. Match the ambition to what the model does reliably, keep the money-moving decision behind a rule and a person, and let evaluation tell you where the line sits for your data. This is the through-line of both major agent guides: give the model the work it handles well, and put a human on the path where a mistake is expensive ([Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/building-effective-agents), [OpenAI](https://cdn.openai.com/business-guides-and-resources/a-practical-guide-to-building-agents.pdf)). To pick the first slice, favor a high-volume repetitive workflow where the clean majority is easy and the exceptions are rare, which is how enterprise teams that scale AI tend to sequence their use cases ([OpenAI, Identifying and Scaling AI Use Cases](https://cdn.openai.com/business-guides-and-resources/identifying-and-scaling-ai-use-cases.pdf)).

Build or buy is a real fork, and finance operations has a mature vendor market. Off-the-shelf AP-automation and expense-audit products (for example AppZen, Stampli, Ramp, Bill.com) get you live faster and carry their own extraction, controls, and reporting. Building in-house gives you control over the policy logic, the data, the audit format, and the cost, and it compounds when your rulebook or your ERP integration is unusual enough that a generic product fits poorly. Decide on a few axes: how unusual your policy and approval chains are, how sensitive the data and the money movement are, token and integration cost against control, time to market against the data and audit trail you would own, vendor lock-in, and whether you have the team to own an evaluation and monitoring loop for the life of the product. A common answer is to buy to learn fast on the standard slice, then build the parts that become differentiating or that a vendor cannot make auditable enough, which mirrors how enterprises that scale AI describe the path ([OpenAI, AI in the Enterprise](https://cdn.openai.com/business-guides-and-resources/ai-in-the-enterprise.pdf)).

> **Real outlier: PwC and Anthropic, 2025.** In production deployments, PwC reported insurance underwriting cycles compressed from about 10 weeks to about 10 days, opening lines of business that were not previously economically viable, with clients across their agentic builds reporting delivery improvements of up to 70%. That is the shape of the win in regulated decisioning: the value is in compressing a slow, judgment-heavy workflow, and the constraint is that these are regulated decisions, so the machinery around the model is the product. [[Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/news/pwc-expanded-partnership)] [[PwC](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/anthropic-pwc-expand-alliance-agentic-enterprise.html)]

### 3 Success metrics

Pick the minimum set that tells you the truth, name the number that must not break, and name the anti-metric you refuse to game.

- **Straight-through-processing rate.** The share of items decided without a human. The core efficiency outcome, and the headline the CFO will ask about.
- **Turnaround time.** How long an invoice or an expense takes from arrival to a decision, and how the month-end backlog behaves. This is what the vendor and the employee feel.
- **False-approve rate and false-decline rate, reported separately.** A blended accuracy number hides the tradeoff that actually matters. A false approve pays a bad, duplicate, or out-of-policy invoice: real money out the door and a compliance exposure. A false decline blocks a legitimate payment: a delayed vendor, a frustrated employee, and analyst rework, which is a real cost and usually a smaller and more recoverable one. Because the costs are asymmetric, you tune the tradeoff on purpose.
- **Incorrect-auto-decision rate.** The hard ceiling, and the number that must not break. It is the share of auto-decided items where the agent got the call wrong, weighted toward false approves because those cost the most. You set this ceiling first and let the straight-through rate be whatever the ceiling allows.
- **Audit completeness.** Every decision carries a verifiable, tamper-evident record of who decided, what, when, and on what evidence. This one is close to binary: a decision without a defensible record is a compliance breach regardless of whether it was correct.
- **Cost per decision and decisions per human touch.** The unit economics finance will price the project on.

Separate leading signals (extraction field accuracy, confidence calibration, escalation precision) from lagging outcomes (turnaround, cost per decision). Pair the efficiency outcome (straight-through rate) with a quality metric beneath it (the incorrect-auto-decision rate) and treat a gap between the two as the alarm, which is the reliability-plus-value framing for agent metrics ([Google Cloud](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/measuring-the-value-of-ai-agents)). The anti-metric to watch is straight-through-processing bought by loosening the auto-approve bar or suppressing routing: an agent that clears the queue by auto-approving items it should have sent to a person looks great on a dashboard and quietly pays bad invoices. Report reliability, because an agent that is right most of the time can still fail often enough on money to be unshippable.

> **Real finding: [tau2-bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.07982).** On a tool-using agent, a single-attempt pass rate looks respectable, and pass^k (succeed on all k independent tries) collapses as k grows. A decisioning agent that reaches the right call on a single try yet fails 1 run in 4 is unshippable when each failure is a mispaid or wrongly blocked invoice. Reliability is the bar for the metric that must not break, above average accuracy.

### 4 The experience: designing for a model that is sometimes wrong

The product is the experience you build around a probabilistic core, and here most of that experience is the analyst's review queue and the record the auditor reads later. Design it so a wrong auto-decision is rare, and so every decision the system does make is legible and recoverable. This has an established design playbook: help the person form an accurate picture of what the agent can do, make its uncertainty legible, and give a fast path to correct it ([Google PAIR People + AI Guidebook](https://pair.withgoogle.com/guidebook/), [Microsoft Guidelines for Human-AI Interaction](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/haxtoolkit/ai-guidelines/)).

- **Show the evidence, always.** Every decision surfaces the extracted fields, the reference matches (vendor, purchase order, prior invoices), and the policy findings that drove it, so the analyst and the auditor can both see why. A decision the finance team cannot explain is a decision they will not trust.
- **Make uncertainty legible and route on it.** When a field the policy needs is low-confidence, or the amount is above the sign-off threshold, or the item is a suspected duplicate, the agent routes to a person with the full context rather than guessing ([designing for AI failure, PAIR](https://pair.withgoogle.com/chapter/errors-failing/)). Route-to-human is the safe default, so anything the system is unsure about lands with an analyst.
- **Set the mental model.** Make it clear to the finance team which decisions the agent makes and which always require a person, so their expectations match what the system does ([mental models, PAIR](https://pair.withgoogle.com/chapter/mental-models/)). The promise that a human signs off on every high-impact case is a product decision made up front.
- **Make correction and override cheap.** Let an analyst override an auto-decision in one step, and route that override back into evaluation as a signal ([feedback and control, PAIR](https://pair.withgoogle.com/chapter/feedback-controls/)). Overrides are among the most valuable data you have for finding what the agent gets wrong.
- **Immutable evidence beats speed.** A decision that lands in a few seconds or a few minutes is fine. Correctness and a defensible, [tamper-evident audit trail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Audit_trail) carry more weight than raw latency, because the record is what makes the whole thing auditable.

### 5 Evaluation and risk

You cannot ship what you cannot measure, and for a money-moving product the evaluation plan is the launch plan. Build a labeled reference set of real items across clean-approve, hard-deny, high-impact, out-of-policy, duplicate, and messy-extraction cases, and treat it as the release gate: a change ships only if false-approve and false-decline rates, escalation precision, and the incorrect-auto-decision ceiling hold. Measure false-approve and false-decline separately, because they cost very different amounts and a blended number hides the tradeoff. This mirrors what we teach in the [AI Evals for Everyone](../../../free_courses/ai_evals_for_everyone/README.md) course, and it is what separates a demo from a product.

Name the one failure that ends the project, and design against it first. Here there are two shapes of it, and both are fatal. The first is a confident wrong auto-approval that pays a bad, duplicate, or out-of-policy invoice, or a payment a document talked the agent into. The second is an unauditable decision: a record that is missing, incomplete, or editable after the fact, so the finance team cannot prove who decided what and on what evidence. Either one loses the trust of the auditors and the CFO, and once that trust is gone the project is done. The guardrails are product-safety features rather than engineering details: the deterministic policy gate means a sentence hidden in an invoice cannot raise a limit, since the limit lives in code the document cannot reach ([prompt injection, OWASP LLM01](https://genai.owasp.org/llmrisk/llm01-prompt-injection/)); human sign-off on high-impact cases means the money path always runs through a person; and the append-only, hash-chained audit record means any tampering is detectable. Together they cap the worst case at a needless routing to a human rather than a wrongful, unexplainable payment.

> **Real outlier: extraction confidence is the whole ballgame.** A 2026 study of LLM-based document field extraction reports frontier models failing on roughly 26% of fields on a 55-field invoice task, and ordinary token-probability confidence separates good extractions from bad ones only weakly. The PM takeaway is that the signal that decides what auto-approves against what an analyst reviews is something you have to engineer and measure, and getting it right is worth as much as the extractor itself. Treat the confidence floor as a lever you tune against the eval set, and track the false-approve rate as you move it. [[Beyond Logprobs](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24420)] [[OHRBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.02592)]

### 6 Rollout and change management

Ship it the way you de-risk any high-stakes launch, in stages with gates, and treat the finance and audit teams as partners you bring along rather than an approval you collect at the end.

- **Shadow first.** Run the agent alongside analysts on real invoices and expenses without letting it post anything, and compare its proposed decisions against the eval bar and against what the analysts actually did.
- **Pilot a slice.** Turn on auto-decisioning for one narrow, low-risk band (for example in-policy invoices below the sign-off threshold from known vendors), read every decision, and watch the metrics from section 3.
- **Bring finance and audit in early.** Walk them through the policy rulebook, the sign-off thresholds, and the audit record before go-live, and let them set the dollar ceiling and the segregation-of-duties rules. The [four-eyes principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_eyes_principle) and [separation of duties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_duties) are controls auditors expect for money movement, so build them in as product features and show the auditors where they live.
- **Stage the rollout** behind go and no-go gates on the incorrect-auto-decision ceiling, audit completeness, and the false-approve rate, with a rollback plan you can trigger in minutes.
- **Monitor and keep a human on the path.** Sample live decisions on signals (analyst overrides, reopened items, disputed payments, items that sat in review too long), watch for drift as vendors and formats change, and guarantee that every high-impact case still reaches a person.

Pull back when the incorrect-auto-decision rate crosses the ceiling, when a wrongful auto-approval reaches a real payment, or when the audit trail fails a verification, and expand the auto-decision envelope only when the eval set on live traffic earns it. You start at high control and move toward higher automation, and every increase in what auto-decides is paid for with an eval that shows the false-approve rate held.

## Follow-ups an interviewer asks

**"How do you measure success, and how do you avoid vanity metrics?"** Lead with straight-through-processing rate paired with the incorrect-auto-decision ceiling, report false-approve and false-decline separately because their costs differ, and refuse to celebrate a straight-through rate that came from loosening the auto-approve bar or suppressing routing. Report reliability, since an agent that is right most of the time can still mispay often enough to erode trust.

**"The agent auto-approves an invoice it should have caught, and it gets paid. Walk me through your response."** Contain the money impact first (recovery, vendor contact, a hold on similar items), read the audit record to trace the failure to the step that broke (a misread field, a stale reference match, or a misapplied rule), add the case to the eval set so it cannot regress, and decide whether it was a one-off or a pattern that should pause the rollout. The immutable audit trail and the human path are what make this recoverable, and the ability to reproduce the decision from the record is why the audit trail was load-bearing from day one.

**"Engineering says 6 months. Scope an MVP."** Cut to one workflow and the clean, in-policy, below-threshold band on known vendors, with the deterministic policy gate, route-to-human as the default, and the immutable audit trail from the first commit. Prove the incorrect-auto-decision rate and audit completeness there before widening the auto-decision envelope or adding a second workflow. The follow-ups add capability the way versions would, without betting the launch on the hardest slice.

**"How do you choose what the agent is allowed to auto-decide?"** Match autonomy to the cost of being wrong. Auto-approve the clean, in-policy, high-confidence, below-threshold majority, auto-deny only unambiguous hard violations, and route everything else (high-impact amounts, low-confidence extractions, suspected duplicates, out-of-policy but plausibly legitimate items) to a person. Move the line outward only when evaluation on live traffic shows the false-approve rate held.

**"The CFO is nervous about a model approving payments. How do you get them comfortable?"** Show that the model never makes the money decision: it reads the document into typed fields, and a deterministic rulebook the auditors can inspect makes the call, with human sign-off on everything high-impact and an append-only record on every decision. Then show the eval bar it had to clear to ship and the rollback plan. Comfort comes from the controls and the evidence being visible, rather than from a promise that the model is accurate.

**"Leadership frames this as headcount reduction. How do you set the goal?"** Frame it as capacity and speed: auto-decide the repetitive clean majority so analysts spend their time on judgment calls and the close lands faster, with the incorrect-auto-decision ceiling and a guaranteed human sign-off on exceptions as guardrails. A pure headcount framing pushes the auto-approve bar past what the false-approve ceiling can safely allow.

**"Build or buy?"** Decide on how unusual your policy and approval chains are, how sensitive the data and the money movement are, and whether you can own an evaluation and audit loop for the product's life. Buy to learn fast on the standard slice, then build the parts that become your advantage or that a vendor cannot make auditable enough.
