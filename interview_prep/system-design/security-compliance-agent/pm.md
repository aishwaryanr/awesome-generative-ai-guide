# Security and Compliance Agent: the PM interview

## The interview question

> "Alert volume is burning out our security team. You are the PM. Should we put an AI agent on the detection and enforcement loop, what does success look like, and how do you avoid both alert fatigue and a missed breach?"

The same scenario as the [engineering version](README.md), worked for an **AI Product Manager** loop. The interviewer here is testing product judgment: whether this should exist, what good looks like, how you de-risk it, and how you make the calls when the model is probabilistic and the stakes are a missed breach on one side and a burned-out security team on the other. The technical design (the layered detector, policy retrieval, guardrails, the audit trail, the harness) lives in the [engineering writeup](README.md). This page is the reasoning of the PM who owns the outcome.

> **Read this with your coding agent.** This case is public. Point Claude Code, Codex, or any agent at `https://github.com/aishwaryanr/awesome-generative-ai-guide/tree/main/interview_prep/system-design/security-compliance-agent` and have it quiz you on the product decisions, or pressure-test your metrics, your precision-recall call, and your rollout plan.

**AI product management is product management with a probabilistic core.** You still start from the user and the business problem, you still pick the smallest thing that moves a real metric, and you still design for failure. What changes is that the core component is non-deterministic, so evaluation, trust, and the human fallback move from edge cases to the center of the product. On a detection-and-enforcement loop that shift is sharper than usual, because the agent watches a live stream and acts on it in the moment, so a wrong call has a consequence the instant it is made.

## The PM spine: 6 questions for any AI product

This is the product companion to the [5-layer engineering spine](../README.md#the-spine-how-we-think-about-ai-system-design). Where the engineer reasons about the model, the wrapping layer, evals, production, and optimization, the PM reasons about whether the thing should exist and how to ship it without breaking the trust of the analysts who have to act on it. Bring these 6 questions to any AI product.

- **1 Problem and user.** Whose pain, what outcome, and is it worth doing at all.
- **2 Fit.** Is AI the right tool, is it good enough yet, and do you build or buy.
- **3 Success metrics.** What you will move, the number that must not break, and the anti-metric you refuse to celebrate.
- **4 Experience.** The probabilistic UX: analyst trust, explainability, and the human handoff on enforcement.
- **5 Evaluation and risk.** How you know it is ready, the guardrails, and the one failure that ends the project.
- **6 Rollout.** Detect-only first, staged gates, go and no-go, and when to pull back.

Product management is composing these into a decision you can defend to a security lead, an engineer, and a CFO in the same meeting.

## The answer

### 1 Problem and user

Start from the problem, and reach for the technology second. This is [Problem-First](https://maven.com/aishwarya-kiriti/genai-system-design), and it is the part a weak PM skips. It is also plain product discipline: classic product discovery de-risks value and viability before a team commits to building, which is the [four big product risks](https://www.svpg.com/four-big-risks/) frame applied to an AI feature.

> **What is Problem-First?** A framework we developed and teach at [LevelUp Labs](https://levelup-labs.ai): work backwards from the business problem and the user's pain, scope hard, and only then reach for AI, choosing the smallest intervention that moves a measurable outcome. It is the spine of our [AI System Design course](https://maven.com/aishwarya-kiriti/genai-system-design).

Frame it in numbers a security org owns. The team watches a firehose of activity (access logs, data movements, configuration changes, transactions) on the order of millions of events a day, while a human reviewer clears a single case in minutes, so most events are never truly reviewed. Two failures run at once, and they pull in opposite directions. Real violations slip through because nobody has time to look, and the alerts that do fire are mostly noise, so the analysts learn to tune them out. The users in pain are the analyst drowning in low-value alerts and the business exposed to the breach that hides in the ones nobody read.

Write the outcome before the system: screen every event on the stream, allow the routine majority automatically, catch the genuine violations, and route the uncertain middle to an analyst with enough context to decide fast. Measure it by the share of real violations caught, the false-positive rate on what reaches a human, the time from event to detection and to response, and whether every flag can be explained and reproduced. Narrow the intervention until it hurts: classify each event as allow, flag, or block, grounded in written policy and the actor's normal behavior, and enforce only the clear cases while a human owns the rest. An agent that tries to cover data-loss, insider threat, fraud, and access review in version 1 is the over-scoping trap.

The clarifying questions you need to ask a stakeholder: what is the event volume and its peak rate; what is the cost of a missed violation, and what is the acceptable false-positive rate on what reaches an analyst; which enforcement actions may the agent take on its own and which need a human; how are the policies written and how often do they change; what must the audit trail prove, and to whom; and what is the current mean time to detect and to respond that you are trying to beat.

> **Real finding: alert fatigue is the failure mode that sinks these systems.** The 2025 SANS Detection and Response survey found that 73% of security teams name false positives their top detection challenge, and false-positive rates in enterprise operations centers frequently exceed 50%, reaching as high as 80% in some organizations. When most alerts lead nowhere, analysts start treating new ones with skepticism and genuine threats get overlooked. That is why the false-positive budget is a product constraint set in scoping rather than a knob you tune later: a detector that cries wolf gets ignored, and an ignored detector catches nothing. [[SANS via Stamus Networks](https://www.stamus-networks.com/blog/what-the-2025-sans-detection-response-survey-reveals-false-positives-alert-fatigue-are-worsening)]

### 2 Fit: is AI the right tool, and is it good enough yet

Capability judgment is the AI PM's signature skill: deciding whether a model belongs anywhere near the problem, and where it earns autonomy. A large share of this decision should stay deterministic. Exact rules (a bulk export of customer records to an external address, an attempt to disable audit logging, a known-bad pattern) are cheaper, faster, and never hallucinate, so they clear the routine majority and block the unambiguous violations without a model call. The model earns its place on the ambiguous middle, where the judgment is too contextual for a fixed rule and too varied to enumerate, and it earns autonomy only where a mistake is cheap. Match the ambition to what the model does reliably, gate the consequential enforcement behind human oversight, and let evaluation tell you where that line sits for your data rather than guessing. This is the through-line of both major agent guides: hand the model the work it handles well, and put a human on the path where a mistake is expensive ([Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/building-effective-agents), [OpenAI](https://cdn.openai.com/business-guides-and-resources/a-practical-guide-to-building-agents.pdf)). To pick the first slice, favor a single high-volume policy domain where the cost of a false positive is a wasted analyst minute rather than a blocked legitimate action, which is how enterprise teams that scale AI tend to sequence their use cases ([OpenAI, Identifying and Scaling AI Use Cases](https://cdn.openai.com/business-guides-and-resources/identifying-and-scaling-ai-use-cases.pdf)).

Build or buy is a real fork, and in security it is sharper because you are deciding against a mature tooling market. Your team already runs a SIEM and detection tooling, and the provider platforms have made agentic security operations the headline of their offerings ([Google Cloud's agentic SOC](https://cloud.google.com/solutions/security/agentic-soc), [Microsoft Security Copilot for the SOC](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/blog/microsoftthreatprotectionblog/security-copilot-for-soc-bringing-agentic-ai-to-every-defender/4470187)). Buying gets you live faster on top of the stack you already own and carries the vendor's own tuning. Building in-house gives you control over which policies the agent grades against, how it explains a flag, what it may enforce autonomously, and how the audit trail is shaped for your regulator, and it compounds where your compliance surface is specific to your business. Decide on a few axes: how specific your policies and controls are to your organization, how sensitive the data and the enforcement actions are, token cost against control, time to market against the tuning you would build on your own event distribution, vendor lock-in, and whether you have the team to own an evaluation and monitoring loop for the life of the product. A common answer is to buy detection to move fast, then build the parts (the policy grading, the enforcement gating, the audit trail) that become differentiating and regulated, which mirrors how enterprises that scale AI describe the path ([OpenAI, AI in the Enterprise](https://cdn.openai.com/business-guides-and-resources/ai-in-the-enterprise.pdf)).

### 3 Success metrics

Pick the minimum set that tells you the truth, name the number that must not break, and name the anti-metric you refuse to celebrate.

- **Detection recall on real violations.** The share of genuine violations the agent catches. A miss is the catastrophic failure, so this is the outcome the whole system exists to move.
- **False-positive rate on what reaches an analyst.** The constraint the design serves. Alert fatigue is documented, so this is the number that must not break: push it up and the analysts stop trusting the queue, and recall on paper stops mattering because nobody reads the flags.
- **Mean time to detect and mean time to respond.** The speed outcomes a security org already reports. Screening every event continuously should pull both down from the periodic-review baseline.
- **Unsafe auto-enforcement rate.** The hard ceiling. An autonomous block on a legitimate action must stay near zero, because a wrongful enforcement action erodes trust faster than a missed alert and can pull the whole program.
- **Analyst-queue volume and clear time.** Too high means the agent is over-flagging and recreating the fatigue you set out to fix; a healthy trend is the sign it is actually helping.

Separate leading signals (grounded-verdict rate, escalation precision) from lagging outcomes (recall confirmed by later investigation, mean time to respond). Pair the business outcome (recall, time to respond) with a quality metric beneath it (false-positive rate, verdict grounding) and treat a gap between the two as the alarm, which is the reliability-plus-value framing for agent metrics ([Google Cloud](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/measuring-the-value-of-ai-agents)). The anti-metric to watch is a clean-looking flag queue bought by an agent that quietly widens what it allows: recall collapses out of sight while the dashboard says false positives are down. Report recall and the false-positive rate together, always, so neither number can be gamed by sacrificing the other.

### 4 The experience: designing for a model that is sometimes wrong

The product is the experience you build around a probabilistic core, and here the primary user is the analyst who has to act on the agent's output. Design it so a wrong call is rare, visible, and cheap to recover from, and so every flag earns the analyst's trust rather than spending it. This has an established design playbook: help the analyst form an accurate picture of what the agent can and cannot judge, make its uncertainty legible, and give a fast path to correct it ([Google PAIR People + AI Guidebook](https://pair.withgoogle.com/guidebook/), [Microsoft Guidelines for Human-AI Interaction](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/haxtoolkit/ai-guidelines/)).

- **Explain every flag.** A flag arrives with its evidence: the event, the policy clause it was graded against, and the behavioral signal that raised it, so an analyst can act in seconds and an auditor can trust the record. An unexplained flag is one an analyst cannot triage, and it becomes noise.
- **Gate on confidence and escalate gracefully.** When the agent cannot ground a verdict or the signal is merely suspicious, it abstains and routes the event to a human with full context rather than forcing an allow or a block ([designing for AI failure, PAIR](https://pair.withgoogle.com/chapter/errors-failing/)). Human review as the default for the uncertain middle is a product decision made up front.
- **Keep enforcement behind a human where it bites.** Autonomous enforcement is limited to clear, policy-named violations, and heavier actions (quarantining an account, revoking access) require human approval. The analyst reviews a case with a recommended action rather than discovering an action already taken, so the agent's mistakes cap at a needless review rather than a wrongful lockout.
- **Set expectations and make correction cheap.** Make it clear what the agent screens for and where it hands off, so the analyst's mental model matches the system ([mental models, PAIR](https://pair.withgoogle.com/chapter/mental-models/)), and let an analyst overturn a verdict in one step, routing that correction straight back into evaluation ([feedback and control, PAIR](https://pair.withgoogle.com/chapter/feedback-controls/)).
- **Latency and load.** Clear violations are caught and enforced in the moment within a tight budget, while a subtle correlation can take a little longer, and the queue is ranked so an analyst sees the highest-severity cases first rather than a flat wall of alerts.

### 5 Evaluation and risk

You cannot ship what you cannot measure, and for an AI product the evaluation plan is the launch plan. Build a labeled set of real events across clear-allow, clear-block, ambiguous, off-baseline, and adversarial cases, each tagged with the destination it should reach and the policy that should govern it, and treat it as the release gate: a change ships only if detection recall, the false-positive rate, escalation precision, and the unsafe-enforcement ceiling all hold. This mirrors what we teach in the [AI Evals for Everyone](../../../free_courses/ai_evals_for_everyone/README.md) course, and it is what separates a demo from a product. Reliability is the bar, above average accuracy: an agent that makes the right call most of the time can still be wrong often enough, across a stream this size, to bury the analyst, which is the [pass^k](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.07982) lesson from tool-agent benchmarks.

The precision-recall tradeoff is the whole risk here, and both ends are project-ending failures. Tighten the agent to catch everything and precision falls, so the analyst drowns in false positives and stops trusting the queue, and an ignored detector catches nothing. Loosen it to keep flags clean and recall falls, so a real breach slips through, which is the catastrophic miss. Name the one failure you design against first, and it is really two faces of the same thing: a confident wrong verdict that either buries the team in noise or waves a genuine violation through. The design answer is to lean toward precision on what gets enforced autonomously and use the human-review route as the release valve, so the uncertain middle goes to a person rather than being forced into an allow or a block, and recall is recovered through the flag path rather than by making the analyst pay for every weak signal. The guardrails (grounding every verdict in a retrieved policy, a relevance floor that escalates when no policy fits, human approval on enforcement, treating the event text as data the agent never obeys) are product-safety features rather than engineering details, because they cap the worst case at a needless escalation.

> **Real data: automation pays, and ungoverned automation is the new risk.** IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach report found that organizations using AI and automation extensively across security saved roughly 1.9 million dollars per breach and cut the breach lifecycle by about 80 days, which is the case for building this at all. The same report found that 97% of organizations that had an AI-related security incident lacked proper AI access controls, and heavy use of unapproved AI added roughly 670 thousand dollars to the average breach. An agent with access to sensitive data and the ability to act is itself a new attack surface, which is why the enforcement gating and the audit trail are load-bearing product requirements rather than engineering polish. [[IBM Cost of a Data Breach 2025](https://www.ibm.com/reports/data-breach)]

### 6 Rollout

Ship it the way you de-risk any high-stakes launch, in stages with gates, and earn autonomy through evaluation before you grant it.

- **Detect-only first, in shadow.** Run the agent on the live stream and have it produce verdicts without enforcing anything, comparing its flags against the eval bar and against what analysts actually escalate. This proves recall and the false-positive rate before the agent can touch a single action.
- **Pilot a slice.** Turn on flagging for one policy domain or one queue, route every flag to an analyst with its evidence, read the overturns, and watch the metrics from section 3. Enforcement stays entirely human in this phase.
- **Enforcement behind approval, then narrow autonomy.** Once the false-positive rate holds, let the agent recommend enforcement for a human to approve, and only then allow autonomous blocks on the clearest, policy-named violations, with heavier actions still gated to a person. Every step outward is paid for with an eval that shows the false-positive rate and the unsafe-enforcement ceiling held.
- **Stage behind go and no-go gates** on detection recall, the false-positive rate, and the unsafe-enforcement ceiling, with a rollback you can trigger in minutes and an audit trail that lets you reconstruct any decision.
- **When to pull back.** If the false-positive rate climbs past budget, if analysts start overturning autonomous verdicts, or if an autonomous enforcement action hits a legitimate user, drop enforcement back to recommend-only or detect-only while you fix, rather than defending a launched capability that is spending analyst trust.

## Follow-ups an interviewer asks

**"How do you measure success, and how do you avoid vanity metrics?"** Lead with detection recall paired with the false-positive rate on what reaches an analyst, report the two together so neither can be gamed by sacrificing the other, and gate on the unsafe-enforcement ceiling. Refuse to celebrate a quiet flag queue that comes from the agent widening what it allows, because that is a hidden recall collapse. Report reliability across the stream, since an agent that is right on average can still fire enough bad calls to get itself ignored.

**"The agent misses a real breach that later causes damage. Walk me through your response."** Contain the incident first, then trace the miss to the step that failed (a rule that never fired, a policy the retrieval did not surface, a verdict the model got wrong, a signal the anomaly baseline had been trained to accept), add the case to the eval set so it cannot regress, and decide whether it was a one-off or a pattern that should tighten the flag threshold. The audit trail is what makes the trace possible, and the human-review route is what should have caught it, so you also check whether the event was wrongly auto-allowed rather than escalated.

**"The agent blocks a legitimate action and disrupts the business. Walk me through it."** This is the mirror failure, and it is why enforcement is gated. Roll the specific enforcement back, notify the affected user, trace why the agent judged a legitimate action as a clear violation, and if it was an autonomous block, pull autonomy back to recommend-only for that policy domain while you fix. A wrongful enforcement action costs more trust than a missed alert, so the bar to auto-enforce is higher than the bar to flag.

**"Engineering says 6 months. Scope an MVP."** Cut to detect-only on one policy domain with deterministic rules plus a model on the ambiguous middle, every flag going to a human, and no autonomous enforcement at all. Prove recall and the false-positive rate there before you add enforcement or a second domain. The follow-ups add autonomy and coverage the way versions would, without betting the launch on the hardest, highest-blast-radius slice.

**"How do you choose the autonomy level?"** Match autonomy to the cost of being wrong and the reversibility of the action. Auto-allow the routine majority, flag the uncertain middle to a human, auto-block only clear policy-named violations whose blast radius is small, and require human approval for anything irreversible like a quarantine or an access revocation. Move the line outward only when evaluation on live traffic earns it.

**"Leadership wants the agent to auto-remediate everything to cut headcount. How do you set the goal?"** Frame it as speed and coverage rather than headcount: screen every event, cut mean time to detect and respond, and hand analysts ranked, explained cases so they spend their time on real threats. The IBM finding is the argument for building it and the caution against ungoverned autonomy in the same breath, so the guardrails and the human path on enforcement are the goal itself rather than an obstacle to it.

**"Build or buy?"** Decide on how specific your policies and enforcement actions are to your organization, how sensitive the data is, and whether you can own an evaluation loop for the product's life. Buy detection to move fast on top of the stack you already run, then build the policy grading, the enforcement gating, and the audit trail that are differentiating and regulated.
